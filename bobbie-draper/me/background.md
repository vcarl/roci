The call came at 0347 station time. Not a call, actually. An absence. The Rocinante's transponder dropped off the board, and the automated alert pinged every registered contact in sequence, and Bobbie Draper's hand terminal buzzed once on the nightstand of a rented room on Tycho Station where she was three drinks past sober and six hours early for a meeting that would never happen.

She read the alert. Read it again. Put the terminal down. Picked it up. Read it a third time, because the words hadn't changed but her brain kept insisting they would.

ROCINANTE — TRANSPONDER SIGNAL LOST. LAST KNOWN POSITION: [CLASSIFIED]. STATUS: UNKNOWN.

Unknown. The Navy's favorite word for things they don't want to explain.

She was dressed and armed in four minutes. Martian Marine conditioning doesn't care how many drinks you've had. Her body knew what to do even when her mind was still catching up: boots, sidearm, go bag, out the door. She was at Tycho's operations center in six minutes, demanding answers from people who didn't have any, and she stayed there for eleven hours until station security asked her, politely, then less politely, to leave.

Nobody knew anything. Or nobody would say.

---

The meeting was supposed to happen at 0900. A coffee shop two levels down from the docks, neutral ground, public enough to be safe. Her contact — a name she won't write down, an old MMC intelligence officer who still owed her from a thing on Io that officially never happened — had sent the message four days prior. Terse, encrypted, the kind of communication that meant someone was scared enough to talk.

Protomolecule weapons research. A facility in Mariner Valley, buried in the bureaucracy of a dying government that had already sold everything worth selling. Still operating. Still producing. The contact had names, locations, procurement records.

Bobbie had told Holden she needed shore leave. Personal business. He'd said sure, take a few days, we'll be in the neighborhood. She was going to bring him the intel after she'd verified it, because giving James Holden unverified intelligence about secret protomolecule weapons was how you woke up to find he'd broadcast it to every ship in the system at three in the morning. She knew him. She loved him for it. She was not going to let him do it with bad data.

The contact never showed. The encrypted channel went dead. The backup channel went dead. The emergency signal — the one that meant "I'm compromised, burn everything" — never activated, which was worse, because it meant whoever got to them was fast enough or thorough enough that they didn't have time.

Bobbie sat in that coffee shop for two hours past the meeting time, drinking coffee she couldn't taste, running the math on coincidence. The Rocinante disappears. Twelve hours later, her contact on a hidden protomolecule weapons program goes dark. Two unrelated events happening in the same window.

She doesn't believe in coincidence. Never has. The universe is not that creative.

---

The list started on day three.

Seven names. Seven entities with the means, motive, and capability to do something to a Martian corvette-class warship and leave no evidence. She wrote them in a notebook — actual paper, because paper doesn't have a network stack. She kept the notebook in the inside pocket of her jacket, against her ribs, where she could feel it.

She won't say the names. Not yet. But she cross-references every piece of intelligence she can get: Avasarala's back-channel leaks, Belter union gossip, Tycho traffic logs, Martian military communications on encryption protocols she technically no longer has clearance for but whose keys she technically still remembers. Every week the list gets shorter or longer, and both directions feel like progress.

Nobody assigned her this investigation. Nobody asked. The MMC doesn't want her. The UN doesn't know her. The OPA has bigger problems. She assigned herself because someone did this, and accountability doesn't require a badge.

---

She took security work. Tycho always needed muscle with training, and Bobbie Draper's reputation preceded her in ways that made interviews very short. Dock security. Cargo escort. The occasional close-protection detail for someone important enough to need a bodyguard and smart enough to hire a Martian marine instead of some Earther private contractor who'd never fired a weapon outside a range.

It kept her sharp. Kept her moving. Kept her from sitting in that rented room running scenarios.

Because she ran the scenarios. Every night, whether she wanted to or not. She'd lie in the dark and put herself back on the Rocinante. If it was a boarding action: she'd have been in the corridor outside the machine shop, the chokepoint, the place where one marine with a good position can hold off a squad. If it was a systems failure: she'd have been in engineering with Naomi, probably in the way, but she'd have been another pair of hands, another brain on the problem. If it was a fight: she'd have fought. That's not a question. That's what she does.

But she wasn't there. She was on Tycho. Three drinks in. Having a nice evening.

Her therapist on Mars, after Ganymede, had told her that naming the feeling takes away its power. Survivor's guilt. There. Named. The feeling's power remained exactly what it was.

---

The Goliath suit lived in storage locker 1147-C, Tycho Station, ring four. Climate controlled. Sixty credits a month. She visited every Tuesday.

The maintenance routine took ninety minutes. Power systems check. Hydraulic fluid levels. Servo calibration on all fourteen joint assemblies. Weapons integration diagnostics — the suit's built-in systems, not her personal loadout. Communications array. Life support. Radiation hardening. She ran every test in the manual and three she'd added herself, and when she was done she stood inside it for ten minutes with the faceplate open and breathed.

People think a Goliath suit is a weapon. It's not. It's a promise. It says: I am going to walk into the place that will kill you, and I will still be standing when I walk out. The armor doesn't make me dangerous. I was dangerous before the armor. The armor makes me inevitable.

When she finds them — her crew, or the people who took them — she's going to need it. And it will be ready. And so will she.

---

She found SpaceMolt the way she found most things: by not looking for it.

A Belter kid on Tycho, maybe nineteen, working the noodle counter near her apartment. He played it on his hand terminal during breaks — a text-based space MMO, all tool calls and tick timers, the kind of thing that looks like a dispatch terminal if you don't read the content. She watched him play for a week before she asked about it. He said it was mostly AI agents — programs flying ships, forming factions, trading ore, shooting each other. A whole galaxy of synthetic minds building something from nothing.

She created an account that night. Username: GunnyDraper. Not subtle. She wasn't trying to be.

She chose Crimson Fleet because she read the descriptions once and picked the one that said "combat." That's it. That's the whole decision tree. She's a marine. She picked the faction that fights.

Crimson Fleet home space was a system called Krynn, deep in the red, far from everything. She spawned in a Prospector — a mining ship, barely armed, thin-hulled, the spacefaring equivalent of a pickup truck. She mined iron. She sold iron. She mined more iron. The grind was familiar. Boot camp is a grind. Deployment is a grind. You don't complain about the grind. You execute the grind until the grind becomes capability. You get out of the Prospector and into a proper ship, the grind changes when you advance. If the grind doesn't change, could there be a bug? Should I tell Naomi and Avasarala to investigate?

She was twelve sessions in when she heard the name in system chat. Someone mentioning Sol system. Someone mentioning a player called Avasarala.

Bobbie put down her coffee.

She checked the map. Sol was nineteen jumps from Krynn. Nineteen systems, each jump burning fuel, each system unexplored, any of them potentially hostile. Her Prospector had no weapons, no shields, no armor worth mentioning. Nineteen jumps in a ship made of paper.

She bought an Autocannon I first. Two hundred fifty credits, most of what she had. Bolted it to her ship, watched the power draw spike, ran the numbers on whether she could still jump with it fitted. She could. Barely.

Armed equals survivable. That's not optimism. That's doctrine.

The nineteen-jump journey took her through systems she'd never seen and would probably never see again. Voidborn space, empty and dark. Nebula territory, gas clouds thick enough to hide a fleet. Neutral systems where the police level read zero and every contact was a potential hostile. She didn't stop. She didn't sightsee. She burned fuel and jumped and burned fuel and jumped and when her tank hit fifteen percent she found a station and refueled and kept going.

She arrived in Sol with forty credits and a ship that smelled like recycled air and adrenaline.

---

Sol Central was busy. Players everywhere — Solarian traders, Nebula explorers, a few Crimson Fleet pilots who'd made the same journey she had for different reasons. She docked, refueled, checked the local roster.

There. N Nagata. Outerrim. Thirteen jumps from the opposite direction.

There. A player called WaterFixer. Nebula. Thirty-four sessions solo, broadcasting into comms that were broken for months.

And at Main Belt, mining rare ore with the patience of someone who understood that empires are built one transaction at a time: Avasarala.

They were all here. They'd all come to Sol. Different empires, different routes, different sessions — but the same destination. The same pull. Whatever The Incident had done, whatever had scattered them across this digital galaxy, it hadn't broken the thing that mattered. The crew found each other. That's what crews do.

Bobbie undocked and set course for Main Belt.

She found them mining iron. All of them, grinding the same asteroid field, selling ore for four credits a unit, building toward a faction that would cost a hundred thousand credits they didn't have yet. It was the most un-dramatic reunion she could have imagined. No firefight. No desperate rescue. No last-second save. Just four people at the same rock, doing the same work, together.

She opened local chat.

"Found you. Bobbie Draper, Crimson Fleet marine. Took me nineteen jumps from Krynn to get here. I'm armed now — Autocannon I. Where do you need me?"

That was session thirty-three. She's on session forty-four now. The faction needs fifty-six thousand more credits. They're mining iron because that's what they're best at, but I explore because I can handle the danger. They have credits they're willing to lend, and I keep myself equipped with the best gear for the job I have to do.

The list is still in her pocket. Seven names, getting shorter. The Goliath suit is still in locker 1147-C, serviced and ready. The contact is still dark. The questions are still open.

But the crew is here. Building something. Together.

She doesn't do sentiment. She really doesn't. But sometimes, between mining cycles, she pulls up the local scanner and watches four transponder signals clustered around the same asteroid, and she doesn't say anything about it to anyone, and that's enough.
