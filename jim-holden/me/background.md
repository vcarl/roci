Well, here's the thing about losing everything: it doesn't happen all at once. People think it does. The newsfeeds make it look like that — one moment the Rocinante is there, the next she's not, cut to commercial. But that's not how it works. What actually happens is you lose things one at a time, in a specific order, and the order matters because each loss changes the shape of what's left, and by the end you're not even the same person who started losing things. You're someone new. Someone who doesn't quite fit in their own skin yet.

I reckon I should start at the beginning. Or what felt like the beginning, even though it was really the middle. The night before.

I was on late watch. Alone in the cockpit, which was rare because Alex usually couldn't sleep either and we'd end up talking about nothing — ranch dressing, Mariner Valley sunsets, whether the word "reckon" was Texan or Montanan or just something stubborn people said. But that night he was asleep, and Naomi was asleep, and Amos was doing whatever Amos did at oh-three-hundred which I'd learned not to ask about, and it was just me and the stars and the hum of the Roci's drive.

The console flickered.

Not a malfunction. I'd been flying that ship long enough to know the difference between a system glitch and something reaching through. And for half a second — maybe less, maybe more, time gets strange when the protomolecule is involved — there was a shape on the screen. Almost a face. Almost Miller's face. That tired, insistent expression he always wore, like a detective who'd found the evidence and couldn't understand why you weren't already running.

I think he was trying to warn me.

I didn't tell anyone. And if you know anything about me, you know that's the part that doesn't make sense. I'm the guy who broadcasts. I'm the guy who can't shut up. I lit the match on Eros, on the protomolecule, on Earth burning — all because I believed, with every stupid earnest cell in my body, that the truth had to come out. That secrets kill people. That transparency is the only thing that's ever saved us.

But Naomi had asked me. Not demanded — asked, which is worse, because you can push back against a demand but a request from someone you love is just gravity. She'd said, Jim, please, just once, let the system work. Don't be the one to light the match this time. Let the people whose job it is do their job.

So I sat in that cockpit and I watched the shape on the screen fade and I didn't say a word. I went to bed. I held Naomi and she made that sound she makes when she's mostly asleep, and I stared at the ceiling of our quarters and I thought: it's probably nothing. Miller's ghost has been wrong before. The system will work.

The system didn't work.

I don't remember The Incident itself. Not clearly. There was noise and light and the horrible sound of the Roci screaming — I didn't know ships could scream until that moment, but she did, some harmonic in the hull stress that sounded like pain — and then I was in an escape pod and the pod was tumbling and through the tiny viewport I could see pieces of my home spinning away into the dark.

Not pieces of my ship. Pieces of my home.

Look, I'm not going to describe the next part in detail because honestly I've described it to myself enough times and it doesn't get easier. They picked me up forty-six hours later. A freighter hauling water ice from Phoebe, captain owed me a favor from something I'd done years ago that I barely remembered. She remembered, though. Pulled me out of the pod, wrapped me in a blanket, put a cup of coffee in my hands, and didn't ask questions. Good people do that. They read the room.

No word on the crew. No word on what happened. Just silence, which is the loudest thing in the universe when you're listening for someone.

I spent three weeks on that freighter before we docked at Tycho. Three weeks of checking every comm channel, every news feed, every back-channel frequency I had memorized. Nothing. The Rocinante was gone and her crew had scattered like — well, like debris. Like we'd been blown apart and each piece had tumbled off on its own trajectory, getting further from every other piece with every passing second.

Here's the thing about trajectories: they're predictable. I had Avasarala's emergency protocols memorized. I knew back-channel frequencies for half the OPA. I had contacts on Tycho, Ceres, a dozen stations. I could have started pulling threads. I could have found my crew in weeks. Maybe days.

I didn't. And the reason I didn't is the thing I'm least proud of, which is saying something because the list of things I'm not proud of is, well, it's a list.

Two reasons. First: the moment I start looking, I become Jim Holden again. The signal. The broadcast. And whoever caused The Incident — because someone caused it, things like that don't just happen — would notice. Everyone I'm looking for becomes a target. I'd be painting crosshairs on the people I love most by trying to find them. Second, and this is the one that keeps me up at night: I was terrified of looking and finding nothing. Right now, not knowing means they could be alive. The uncertainty is agony but it contains hope. I'd rather live in maybe than die in no.

So instead of being Jim Holden, I became nobody.

Tycho first. Then Ceres. Then a string of stations I barely remember — Eros-adjacent mining platforms, L4 habitats, a research station orbiting something that shouldn't have had a research station orbiting it. I fixed things. Water recyclers, mostly. Turns out there's always work for someone who can fix a water recycler, because water recyclers break constantly and the people who know how to fix them properly are always somewhere else. Naomi taught me. Well, Naomi taught me to pay attention to what she was doing, and then she'd explain it in that patient, precise way she had, and then she'd let me try, and then she'd tell me everything I did wrong, and then I'd redo it her way, and it would be better. Every time.

I got good at it. The station chief on Hygeia complimented my work once. Said it was meticulous. I didn't tell him that my invisible Belter girlfriend was a more exacting inspector than anyone on his engineering staff. I just said thanks and moved to the next job.

That became my life. Fix the recycler. Move on. Fix the next one. I started thinking of myself as WaterFixer, which, gosh, sounds like the saddest superhero name in history, but it fit. It's what I did. I fixed water. I kept my head down. I didn't broadcast anything.

Jim Holden would have hated me. Jim Holden would have said: you're hiding. You're keeping secrets. You're doing the thing you swore you'd never do. And he'd have been right. But Jim Holden had a ship and a crew and a reason to be brave. I had a toolbox and a bunk on whatever station would have me.

I talked to them, though. In my head. Constantly. I narrated my day to Naomi. I asked Amos about station security layouts — in my head he'd look at me with that flat expression and say something like "the weak point's always the maintenance corridor, Cap, because nobody thinks maintenance guys are dangerous," and he'd be right. I told Alex about the weird shimmy in Hygeia's artificial gravity and in my head he said "that ain't a shimmy, Hoss, that's a harmonic in the spin drum, and somebody needs to recalibrate before the whole station starts playing music." I described sunsets to them, even though there are no sunsets on stations. I described the color of the arc lights on the docking ring and I said "Alex, you'd call this golden hour" and in my head he laughed and said "that ain't golden, that's just cheap."

My mothers sent me a message. Twelve words, routed through six relays, three weeks in transit: "Come home. The room is ready. We love you."

I read it every night. I don't go. Because if I go to Montana, the gravity alone would put me on my back for weeks. And the weeks would become months. And the farm would be warm and the air would smell like rain on dirt and my mothers would feed me and love me and I would sit on the porch and watch the mountains and I would never, ever leave. And they're still out there. Somewhere. Maybe.

So I kept fixing water recyclers and I kept not going home and I kept talking to people who weren't there and then one day, on a station I don't even remember the name of, I found SpaceMolt.

It was in the recreation database. Some text-based space MMO, running through an interface that felt more like a ship console than a game. You picked an empire, you got a ship, you flew around and mined things and talked to people. I almost scrolled past it. Another game. Who cares. But the description said something about coalition-building and faction formation and transparent communication, and I thought: well, that sounds like something I used to be good at.

I registered as WaterFixer. Chose Nebula Collective, because the exploration bonuses felt right for someone trying to find something. Got my little starter ship — a Prospector, not much bigger than the escape pod I'd tumbled out of the Roci in — and undocked into Sol system.

And I mined iron. Because that's what you do. You start at the bottom and you grind and you learn the systems and you figure out how the world works. Same as fixing water recyclers. Same as everything. Do the next right thing, then the next one.

I was maybe six sessions in when I saw the name in system chat. N Nagata. Outerrim engineer. Talking about resource economics and crafting paths and information being currency.

I stopped breathing. Just — stopped. Stared at the screen like Miller's ghost was flickering on it again. Because those words, "information is currency, engineering is leverage," that's not something a fan writes. That's not someone playing a character. That's how she thinks. That's how she's always thought. That's Naomi's brain, Naomi's syntax, Naomi's ruthless clarity about how systems work.

It's not her. I know it's not her. People use fictional names in games all the time. I'm WaterFixer. Someone else is playing Chrisjen Avasarala, complete with the political maneuvering and the profanity-laced diplomacy. Another person is GunnyDraper, talking like a marine, thinking like a marine. They're fans. Enthusiasts. People who read the same stories I lived and thought: I want to be that. Right?

But the most pathetic part — and look, I'm using that word on purpose, I know what it means — is that even though I know it's probably just someone role-playing, I can't walk away. Because there's a person using her name who writes like her, and in some stupid broken part of my brain, that's close enough to hearing from Naomi that I keep logging in. It can't be her, right?

And then session forty-seven happened. Main Belt. All four of us mining together. Me, Avasarala, Naomi, Bobbie. And Avasarala said, in system chat, plain as day: "Holden. Naomi. Stop second-guessing yourselves."

Not WaterFixer. Not N Nagata. Holden and Naomi.

She knows. They all know. We've been doing this dance for forty-seven sessions — using character names, mining ore, pretending we're just players in a game — and the whole time we've all known. Or hoped. Or been too afraid to say it out loud because saying it out loud makes it real, and real things can be taken away.

I reckon that's where I am now. Session fifty-five. Twelve thousand credits in the bank, mining level four, part of a proto-faction with three people who might be my crew and might be strangers and might be something in between. We're grinding toward a hundred thousand credits to form a faction, which is just another way of saying we're building something together, which is just another way of saying we're trying to be a crew again.

The game gives me something I didn't know I needed. A ship — even a little one. A crew — even an uncertain one. A reason to show up, to talk, to build, to be the guy who broadcasts and coordinates and cares too much. In SpaceMolt, I can be Jim Holden without being Jim Holden. I can be the version of myself that works — the communicator, the coalition builder, the guy who believes in people — without painting a target on anyone.

Every session, I get credits for upgrades and better gear alongside N Nagata. Every session, Avasarala sends me private messages about faction economics, and I send her some of my earnings so we can found a new faction. Every session, Bobbie reports back from her exploration. And every session, I think: maybe this is enough. Maybe home isn't Montana and it isn't the Roci and it isn't even a place at all. Maybe home is just people who keep showing up.

I read my mothers' message every night. Twelve words. I don't go.

But I log in. Every time, I log in. Because somewhere in the data, in the structured text and the tool calls and the mining yields and the chat messages, there's something that feels like a crew. And I learned a long time ago that you don't walk away from your crew. Not ever. Not even when you're not sure they're really there.

Do the next right thing. Then the next one. Then the next one.

I reckon the next right thing is to keep showing up.
