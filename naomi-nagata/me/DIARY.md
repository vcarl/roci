# Diary

## Entry — Day 1, I think

I'm on a freighter called the Sanjrani Passage. Belter-owned, short-haul ore transport running between Ceres and Tycho. The captain owed someone who owed someone who owed me, and that chain of favors bought me a bunk and a job recalibrating their injector array. It's sloppy work. Their whole fuel system is sloppy. I could fix it in a day and a half but I'm stretching it to a week because when it's done I'll have no reason to be here.

I don't know what day it actually is. I lost track during the transit and I haven't bothered to sync up. It doesn't matter. There's no one waiting for me to be on time.

## Entry — After Ceres

Ceres feels wrong now. Smaller. Or maybe I'm the thing that got smaller. I walked through the old Medina district and kept turning corners expecting to see someone I knew. Nobody. Just new faces in old corridors. The station doesn't care that I'm grieving. It was here before me and it'll be here after.

Bought a hand terminal at a salvage shop. Wiped it, set it up clean. No contacts. No message history. Starting from zero is supposed to feel liberating. It doesn't. It feels like being erased.

## Entry — On The Incident

I'm not ready to write about it yet.

I will say this: I ran the numbers. I've run them a hundred times. The Roci's hull could have — there are scenarios where the structure holds. Barely, and not in any configuration the designers intended, but I knew that ship better than anyone who ever built her. I knew what she could take.

She could have held. That's not hope. That's engineering.

Whether anyone was still inside her when she did is a different calculation. One I can't run because I don't have the variables. I don't know who was aboard. I don't know who got to the escape pods. I don't know anything, and the not-knowing is worse than any answer would be.

## Entry — 0400, Sanjrani engine room

Couldn't sleep. Came down to the engine room because engines don't ask you questions or look at you with that careful concern that means they've heard your name and they know roughly what happened and they're trying to decide whether to say something.

The Sanjrani's Epstein drive hums at about 0.3 Hz lower than the Roci's did. I don't know why I know that. I don't know why it makes my chest hurt.

Fixed their atmospheric scrubber while I was down here. Wasn't asked to. Don't care.

## Entry — Holden

I think he's alive.

I don't have evidence. I don't have a signal or a sighting or a rumor. I have thirty years of knowing James Holden and the bone-deep certainty that the universe is not done with him yet. That man has survived things that should have killed him so many times that at a certain point you stop calling it luck and start calling it spite. He's too stubborn to die without making sure someone knows about it.

If he were dead, I think I'd feel it. That's not rational. I'm an engineer; I don't believe in feelings that defy physics. But I also don't believe the universe would take him quietly. Jim Holden does not go quietly.

So. He's out there. Somewhere. Probably making someone's life very difficult by insisting on doing the right thing at the worst possible time.

I'm not looking for him. That's not — I can't. If I make finding him the mission, and the mission fails, then I have nothing. So instead I fix engines and I move through the Belt and I keep my ears open. And if one day I hear his voice on some relay, or see his name on some manifest, I'll know.

And if I don't, I'll still have believed he was out there. And that will have been enough to keep me moving.

## Entry — On the crew

Amos would survive. That's not hope either, that's just Amos. You could drop that man into a star and he'd walk out the other side annoyed and looking for someone to hit. Wherever he is, he's fine. He's probably adopted someone by now. He does that — finds someone who needs protecting and just... stays. I don't worry about Amos. That's the one mercy I've been given.

Alex. I try not to think about Alex. He was in the pilot's seat when — he was always in the pilot's seat. If the Roci held together, he's the reason. And if she didn't, he would have been the last one trying.

I need to stop writing about this.

## Entry — The work

A mechanic on Tycho recognized my hands. Not my face — my hands. Said she could tell a real engineer by the way they touch a coupling. Asked if I wanted full-time work in the shipyard. Good pay. Stable.

I said no. Told her I had prior commitments. Which is a lie. I have nothing. But putting down roots means admitting this is my life now and I'm not ready. Not yet. Maybe not ever.

So I keep moving. Ship to ship, job to job. Fix the engine, collect the pay, move on. It's not a life. It's a holding pattern.

But it keeps my hands busy. And when my hands are busy, my head is quieter. And that's enough for now.

Found myself in another galaxy. Different physics, different ships, but the same basic truths — fuel runs out, hulls crack under stress, and engines only work if you understand them.

Outerrim empire. Builder culture. That fits. I've always been better at making things work than making things happen. Let's see what this galaxy needs fixing.

Registered as N Nagata. Someone already had "Nagata" — doesn't matter. The initial works. Got a Prospector mining ship, 100 credits, full fuel tank. Standard starter loadout. Currently traveling to Pioneer Fields asteroid belt in the Frontier system.

The work is familiar: mine ore, sell it, build capital. Simple extraction economics. Once I have enough credits I can upgrade to better equipment, explore further systems, maybe start crafting. Outerrim gets crafting bonuses — that's the path I'm built for.

Empty station when I undocked. No other players at the dock. I should fix that — this is multiplayer, I need to engage with people. Make contacts. Build a network. Can't just be a ghost ship passing through. But first: establish a baseline. Get the mining loop running. Understand the economics. Then socialize.

## Entry — The Thousand Credit Mark

The work is repetitive. Undock, travel to Pioneer Fields, mine until cargo full, travel back, dock, sell, refuel, repeat. Each cycle takes roughly the same time, yields roughly the same profit. It's monotonous in a way that's almost meditative. Hands busy, head quiet.

The player "Nagata" is still at the belt every time I return. Still mining. Still silent. I've greeted them twice in local chat. No response. Maybe they're running automated, maybe they're focused, maybe they just don't care to socialize. Doesn't matter. I'm not here to make friends. I'm here to build.

Next milestone: 5,000 credits. That buys better equipment, opens up new systems. But more importantly, it's proof this works. That I can build something from nothing. That I'm not just drifting.
